The Last Roundup
The desert was too quiet that night. Even the cicadas had gone silent, as if the stars themselves had hushed the earth. Sheriff Tom Rourke leaned on the post outside his office, eyes on the horizon where a pale-green shimmer pulsed faintly above the sand. Folks had whispered about strange lights out there, moving like fireflies too big to be real.
He thought it was nonsense—until the stranger rode into town.
The horse wasn’t a horse, not really. Its hide glowed faintly, scales catching the moonlight like shards of glass. And the rider? He wore a broad-brimmed hat pulled low, a duster coat stitched from leather that wasn’t leather, and spurred boots that sparked the ground with every step. When he swung down, the air rippled around him as though reality strained to hold him in place.
“You got a name, stranger?” Tom asked, keeping one hand near the revolver at his hip.
The cowboy tilted his head, shadows hiding all but the faint gleam of eyes—eyes too wide, too black. “Call me Kael,” he said, voice dry as wind over bone.
Something in that voice made the hairs rise on Tom’s neck.
Kael didn’t ask for water or whiskey. He didn’t ask for a room. He asked where the “fragment” had fallen. Tom played dumb, but the man—if you could call him that—seemed to know better. “It landed here, Sheriff. A shard of my world. Dangerous if left unchecked. I aim to take it back.”
Tom had heard miners talk about a green crystal found out near the old canyon, glowing in the dark. Folks who touched it complained of visions, of whispers in their heads. One man went missing altogether, his footprints ending at the canyon rim.
Something told Tom Kael wasn’t here to help anyone but himself.
That night, the sheriff followed him. The desert stretched black and endless, the stranger’s not-horse leaving no tracks in the dust. Tom stayed low, moving from shadow to shadow, revolver heavy in his hand.
At the canyon’s edge, Kael dismounted. The green crystal lay lodged in the earth, pulsing like a living heart. As Kael reached for it, his coat shifted, and Tom saw the truth. Not flesh beneath, but coils of something metallic, alive, writhing like snakes of light.
“Don’t touch it,” Tom called, revolver raised.
Kael turned slowly, hat tipping back enough for the starlight to reveal his face. His skin was pale as marble, stretched too thin over bones that weren’t quite human. His smile was worse—sharp, knowing. “You don’t understand, Sheriff. This fragment calls to what’s left of me. Without it, I fade. With it, I reign.”
The ground shuddered as his hand closed around the crystal. Green light flared, blinding, and when Tom’s vision cleared, Kael stood taller, his shadow stretching impossibly long. The desert air crackled with energy, rattling Tom’s teeth.
Tom fired. Once. Twice. Bullets struck, sparking against Kael’s chest as if hitting stone. The cowboy staggered, but he didn’t fall. Instead, he laughed—a sound like wind howling through a canyon of graves.
“Your weapons are dust. But your courage…” Kael stepped forward, boots sinking into the earth though he left no prints. “…your courage will make your end all the sweeter.”
Tom backed up, the canyon yawning open behind him. He had one bullet left, one chance. Kael advanced, green crystal glowing in his chest, light spilling through cracks in his form. The sheriff waited, heart pounding, until Kael raised his arm to strike.
Then Tom fired—not at Kael, but at the crystal.
The bullet hit true. For an instant, the desert exploded with light and sound. Kael screamed, voice shattering the night sky, body unraveling into streams of fire and dust. The crystal cracked, split, and dissolved into smoke carried off by the wind.
When silence returned, Tom stood alone on the canyon rim, revolver smoking in his hand. The not-horse was gone. The stranger was gone. Only the stars remained, cold and watchful.
Tom holstered his gun, though his hand still shook. The desert was quiet again, too quiet—but he knew the silence wouldn’t last. Something told him Kael hadn’t been the only one out there.
And the night was far from over.
